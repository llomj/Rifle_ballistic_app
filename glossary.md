# Rifle Ballistic App — Glossary

Comprehensive reference for rifles, scopes, bullets, ballistics, ammunition, environment, and related terms used in the app.

---

## Rifles & Firearms

- **Rifle**: A long-barrelled firearm designed to be fired from the shoulder, with spiral grooves (rifling) inside the barrel to spin the projectile for stability and accuracy.
- **Bolt-action**: A mechanism where the shooter manually cycles the bolt to chamber a round, lock the breech, fire, extract, and eject. Favoured for accuracy and reliability (e.g. Tikka T3, Remington 700).
- **Lever-action**: A repeating action operated by a lever (often under the stock) that cycles the next round. Common in hunting and cowboy-style rifles (e.g. Winchester 1894, Marlin 336).
- **Straight-pull**: A bolt that is cycled by pulling straight back and pushing forward (no rotation). Faster than traditional bolt rotation (e.g. Blaser R8, Merkel Helix).
- **Caliber**: The nominal internal diameter of the barrel (or bullet diameter), usually in inches (e.g. .308) or millimetres (e.g. 7.62 mm). Often used to name the cartridge (e.g. .308 Winchester).
- **Barrel length**: The length of the rifled tube from breech to muzzle. Affects muzzle velocity, handling, and balance. Typical rifle barrels: 50–66 cm (20–26 in).
- **Twist rate**: How fast the rifling spins the bullet, expressed as one turn per so many inches (e.g. 1:10 = one turn in 10 inches). Faster twist stabilizes heavier/longer bullets.
- **Chamber**: The rear part of the barrel that holds the cartridge when the action is closed. Must match the cartridge exactly.
- **Breech**: The rear end of the barrel where the cartridge is loaded; the part that is closed by the bolt or action.
- **Muzzle**: The front opening of the barrel from which the bullet exits.
- **Action**: The mechanism that loads, locks, fires, and ejects cartridges. Types include bolt, lever, semi-automatic, straight-pull.
- **Receiver**: The main body of the rifle that houses the action and to which barrel, stock, and magazine attach.
- **Stock**: The part held against the shoulder and often the cheek; can be wood or synthetic. Includes buttstock and forend.
- **Trigger**: The mechanism the shooter presses to release the firing pin or striker and fire the round.
- **Magazine**: A container that holds cartridges and feeds them into the chamber. Can be internal (fixed) or detachable.
- **Safety**: A device that blocks the trigger or firing mechanism to prevent accidental discharge.

## Scopes & Optics

- **Riflescope**: A telescopic sight mounted on the rifle to magnify the target and provide an aiming reference (reticle).
- **Magnification**: How much the scope enlarges the image (e.g. 3–9× means 3× to 9× zoom). Fixed magnification (e.g. 6×) has no zoom.
- **Objective lens**: The front lens that gathers light. Larger diameter (e.g. 42 mm, 50 mm) generally allows more light and a wider field of view.
- **Ocular lens**: The eyepiece lens you look through. Often has a diopter or focus ring.
- **Tube diameter**: The main body diameter of the scope (e.g. 1 inch, 30 mm, 34 mm). Larger tubes allow more elevation/windage adjustment and often more robust construction.
- **Focal plane**: Where the reticle is placed in the optical system. **FFP** = First Focal Plane (reticle scales with magnification). **SFP** = Second Focal Plane (reticle subtension changes with magnification; usually correct at one magnification, e.g. max).
- **FFP (First Focal Plane)**: Reticle is in front of the magnification lenses. Mil/MOA spacing is correct at every magnification; subtensions stay constant.
- **SFP (Second Focal Plane)**: Reticle is behind the magnification lenses. Subtensions are accurate only at a stated magnification (often max); at other magnifications, holdover/mil values must be scaled.
- **Reticle**: The crosshair or pattern in the scope used for aiming and measuring. Types include duplex, mil-dot, BDC, Christmas tree.
- **Duplex**: A simple reticle with thick outer lines that taper to a thin centre crosshair. Good for quick aim in low light.
- **Mil-Dot**: Reticle with dots (or similar marks) spaced at milliradian intervals, used for range estimation and holdover.
- **BDC (Bullet Drop Compensator)**: Reticle or turret marked for holdover at set distances for a given load; simplifies shooting at known ranges.
- **Christmas tree / grid reticle**: Reticle with horizontal and sometimes vertical marks (like a tree) for wind and drop at various distances (e.g. Horus, EBR-2C).
- **Elevation turret**: Knob (usually on top) that adjusts the point of impact up or down. May be capped or exposed (tactical).
- **Windage turret**: Knob (usually on the side) that adjusts point of impact left or right.
- **Click value**: The change in point of impact per click of the turret (e.g. 1/4 MOA, 0.1 MIL). Must be known for accurate adjustments.
- **Clicks per revolution**: How many clicks in one full turn of the turret. With click value, this determines total adjustment per revolution.
- **Zero stop**: A mechanical stop so the turret can be returned to the zero setting (e.g. after dialling for long range) without counting clicks.
- **Parallax**: Optical effect where the reticle appears to move relative to the target if the eye moves. Parallax adjustment (side or objective knob) corrects it at a chosen distance.
- **Eye relief**: Distance from the ocular lens at which you see the full image. Critical to avoid scope bite; must be sufficient for the rifle’s recoil.
- **Field of view (FOV)**: The width of the scene seen through the scope at a given distance, often in metres at 100 m or feet at 100 yards.
- **Exit pupil**: Diameter of the light beam leaving the eyepiece. Larger exit pupil (e.g. 5 mm+) is easier in low light. Approximate formula: objective diameter ÷ magnification.

## Angular Units (MIL, MOA, MRAD)

- **MIL (milliradian)**: One thousandth of a radian. 1 MIL ≈ 1 m at 1000 m (3.6 in at 100 y). Used for range estimation and holdover (e.g. distance = target size × 1000 / mils).
- **MRAD**: Same as MIL; milliradian. Common on European and tactical scopes.
- **MOA (Minute of Angle)**: 1/60 of a degree. ≈ 1.047 in at 100 yards, ≈ 2.9 cm at 100 m. Used for adjustments and group size.
- **TMOA (True MOA)**: 1/60 of a degree, geometric. Slightly larger than “shooter MOA” (1 in at 100 y).
- **SMOA (Shooter MOA)**: Defined as 1 inch at 100 yards for simplicity. Slightly smaller than true MOA.
- **Conversion**: 1 MIL = 1 mrad ≈ 3.438 MOA. Scope and app must use a consistent system (MIL/MRAD or MOA).

## Bullets & Projectiles

- **Bullet**: The projectile (not the whole cartridge) fired from the barrel. Can be lead, jacketed, monolithic, or hybrid.
- **Cartridge**: The complete round: case, primer, powder, and bullet. Often loosely called “bullet” or “round.”
- **Case**: The brass (or other) container that holds primer, powder, and bullet. Provides obturation and extraction.
- **Caliber (bullet)**: Bullet diameter, e.g. .308 in, 7.62 mm. Must match the barrel’s bore and twist for stability.
- **Weight (bullet)**: Usually in grains (gr) or grams (g). Heavier bullets often have higher BC and better long-range performance but lower muzzle velocity for same pressure.
- **Ballistic Coefficient (BC)**: Measure of how well the bullet resists air drag compared to a standard reference. Higher BC = less drop and drift. Depends on drag model (e.g. G1, G7).
- **G1 BC**: Ballistic coefficient referenced to the G1 standard projectile. Common in older data and many factory listings.
- **G7 BC**: Referenced to a longer, boat-tail standard. Often better represents modern long-range rifle bullets.
- **Drag model**: Mathematical model of how drag varies with speed (G1, G2, G5, G6, G7, G8, GL). G1 and G7 are most used for rifles.
- **Boat tail**: Tapered base of the bullet that reduces drag and can improve BC.
- **Flat base**: Bullet base that is flat. Simpler and often used in short-range or hunting; typically higher drag than boat tail.
- **Spitzer**: Pointed (sharp) nose shape for lower drag and flatter trajectory.
- **Round nose**: Rounded nose; more drag, often used for short range or certain game.
- **Soft point (SP, JSP, PSP)**: Jacketed bullet with exposed lead tip. Expands on impact; common for hunting.
- **Hollow point (HP, HPBT)**: Cavity in the nose (or tip). HPBT = Hollow Point Boat Tail, often used for match or hunting.
- **Polymer tip**: Plastic tip over a cavity; improves expansion and often BC (e.g. Ballistic Tip, SST, ELD-X).
- **Bonded**: Jacket and core bonded to limit separation and increase weight retention (e.g. Accubond, Swift A-Frame).
- **Monolithic**: Single material (e.g. copper alloy); no lead core. Good penetration and often used where lead is restricted (e.g. Barnes TSX/TTSX, Hornady CX).
- **Match bullet**: Designed for consistency and accuracy (e.g. Sierra MatchKing, ELD-Match, Berger Hybrid). Often HPBT or similar.
- **Ogive**: The curved portion of the bullet between nose and bearing surface. Affects BC and seating depth.

## Ammunition & Loading

- **Muzzle velocity**: Speed of the bullet at the muzzle (m/s or ft/s). Key input for ballistic calculators.
- **Chambering**: The cartridge the rifle is chambered for (e.g. .308 Winchester, 6.5 Creedmoor). Must match the ammunition.
- **Grain (gr)**: Unit of mass for bullets and powder. 1 grain ≈ 0.0648 g. 7000 grains = 1 pound.
- **Powder**: Propellant that burns to create gas and propel the bullet. Type and charge weight affect velocity and pressure.
- **Primer**: Small cap that ignites the powder when struck by the firing pin. Boxer (one flash hole) and Berdan (two holes) are common.
- **Hand load / handload**: Ammunition assembled by the shooter (reloading). Allows custom bullet, powder, and velocity.
- **Factory load**: Commercially loaded ammunition. Velocity and BC are typically published or measurable.

## Ballistics & Trajectory

- **Trajectory**: The path of the bullet from muzzle to target under gravity and drag.
- **Bullet drop**: Vertical distance the bullet falls below the line of sight (or bore line) at a given range. Increases with distance.
- **Zero / zero distance**: The range at which the line of sight and the trajectory are set to cross (e.g. 100 m, 200 m). Often chosen as “zero range.”
- **Point-blank range**: Range over which the bullet stays within a certain vertical window (e.g. ±5 cm) without holdover. Depends on zero and trajectory.
- **Holdover**: Aiming above the target to compensate for drop at longer range. Can be in MIL, MOA, or reticle marks.
- **Hold under**: Aiming below the target when target is closer than zero.
- **Elevation adjustment**: Turret or reticle change to raise or lower point of impact. Expressed in MIL/MOA or clicks.
- **Windage**: Horizontal adjustment or hold for wind (or mechanical zero).
- **Wind drift**: Horizontal displacement of the bullet caused by crosswind. Depends on time of flight, wind speed, and BC.
- **Time of flight**: Duration from muzzle to target. Affects drop and wind drift.
- **Energy (muzzle / at range)**: Kinetic energy of the bullet (e.g. in joules or ft·lbf). Often used for terminal effect or legal minimums.
- **Recoil**: Rearward force on the shooter from the bullet and propellant gases. Affects follow-up shots and comfort.

## Range & Environment

- **Target height / size**: The apparent or known dimension of the target used in the range formula (e.g. shoulder width of game, height in mils).
- **Range estimation (mils)**: Distance = (target size × 1000) / mils when size is in the same units as distance (e.g. metres). Formula assumes correct subtension (e.g. FFP or SFP at correct mag).
- **Range estimation (MOA)**: Distance = (target size × 95.5) / MOA (approx.) with size and distance in consistent units.
- **Atmosphere**: Air density affects drag and thus trajectory. Modelled by temperature, pressure (or altitude), and optionally humidity.
- **Altitude**: Height above sea level. Higher altitude usually means less dense air, less drag, less drop.
- **Temperature**: Colder air is denser; bullet slows more and drops more. Hotter air has the opposite effect.
- **Pressure (barometric)**: Higher pressure = denser air. Often derived from altitude or a barometer in advanced solvers.
- **Humidity**: Slight effect on air density; sometimes included in precision solvers.
- **Density altitude**: Altitude corrected for temperature (and sometimes humidity). Single number that approximates “effective” air density for performance.

## Targets & Shooting

- **Target**: Object or animal at which the shooter aims. In the app, “target height” is the dimension used for range or holdover.
- **Target height library**: Preset or user-defined sizes (e.g. deer chest, human, steel plate) for quick range or holdover.
- **Steel target**: Metal plate used for practice; size and distance can be used for range and verification.
- **Group**: Pattern of bullet impacts on a target. Group size (e.g. in MOA or mm) indicates precision.
- **Zeroing**: Process of adjusting scope or sights so point of impact matches point of aim at the chosen zero distance.

## App-Specific Terms

- **Rifle profile**: Saved set of rifle data (caliber, barrel length, twist, scope height, zero distance, etc.) in the app.
- **Scope settings**: Stored scope parameters: FFP/SFP, unit (MIL/MOA), click value, clicks per revolution, magnification (for SFP).
- **Quick Range mode**: Flow where user enters target height and mils (or MOA) to get distance, then holdover and/or clicks.
- **Ballistic table**: Table of distance vs. drop, holdover (MIL/MOA), and turret clicks for the current rifle/load.
- **Turret clicks**: Number of elevation (or windage) clicks to dial for a given distance or drop.
- **Scope height above bore**: Vertical distance from the centre of the bore to the centre of the scope. Affects near-zero trajectory and solver accuracy.
- **Click value (app)**: The angular change per click (e.g. 0.1 MIL, 1/4 MOA) entered in scope settings; used to convert drop to clicks.
